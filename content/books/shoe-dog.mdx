## Why This Book Matters

This isn't your typical business book. Phil Knight wrote this memoir when he was nearly 80, and it reads like a novel — full of self-doubt, near-bankruptcy, broken relationships, and a stubborn refusal to quit.

## The Parts That Stuck

### The early days were chaos

Nike didn't start as a billion-dollar vision. It started as a guy selling shoes out of the trunk of his car at track meets. Knight was terrified, broke, and constantly on the edge of financial ruin. The company almost died multiple times in its first decade.

> I'd tell men and women in their mid-twenties not to settle for a job or a profession or even a career. Seek a calling. Even if you don't know what that means, seek it.

### Relationships matter more than strategy

The early Nike team — Jeff Johnson, Bob Bowerman, the other "shoe dogs" — were bound together by something deeper than equity or job titles. They genuinely believed in what they were doing. The loyalty and weirdness of that early team is what carried the company.

### Vulnerability in leadership

Knight is incredibly honest about his failures as a boss, a husband, and a father. He doesn't pretend to have had all the answers. That vulnerability makes the successes feel earned rather than inevitable.

## What I Think

One of the best memoirs I've ever read. It demolished my idea of what "founding a company" looks like. It's messy, scary, deeply personal, and often more about endurance than brilliance. If you only read one business memoir, make it this one.
